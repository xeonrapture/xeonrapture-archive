# Intro

So you've gotten a group of friends together and play a game in XRRPG, but you don't quite know where to start? This document will run you through the steps you'll need to go through in order to get a game set up, including what a Session 0 should look like and what you'll need to have prepared before that Session 0.

# First Steps

## 1: Talk to Your Players

The first thing you need to do when making a game in the XRRPG is talk to your players! Ask them what kind of game they want to play. What should the tone be, or the aesthetic? Should their characters be hardy and ready to get hurt and make hard decisions, or should they be soft, silly, and fun? How comfortable is the party with PC death?

These questions will determine whether your game is sci-fi, fantasy, or a blend; whether it's fun and comedy-filled, heroic and sweeping, or grimdark and gritty; whether you want to put down roots in one area and explore it deeply or hop around from place to place and from adventure to adventure.

Keep in mind that playing a collaborative game like this is a conversation: your players may not want to play a game that's exactly what you have in mind. This might mean that you and your players are not the right fit for one another (and that's a totally acceptable conclusion, if that's what works for you and your players), or it might mean that everybody needs to be patient with one another at times while everyone includes a bit of what they want in their game.

Ask your players what their ideal game would look like. What parts of RPGs do they have the most fun with? Battle, social interaction, puzzles, dungeoneering, lore, heists? Questions like this make it easier for you to tailor an experience that will be fun for everyone.

Just communicate! Odds are you and your group can figure something out that works pretty well for everyone, and keeping channels of communication open throughout the entire game can help you work through the issues that almost always arise in big group games like RPGs.

It's also important to understand what everyone in the party is comfortable with. Some parties will be fine with gore, violence, swearing, sex, etc. and others won't want these things in their game at all. Just make sure everyone is on the same page so nobody gets blindsided by an adventurous choice.

There are also a ton of great resources on the internet about what kinds of things you should clear up before starting a game and how to resolve issues that may arise ([click here](https://www.reddit.com/r/dndnext/comments/601awb/session0_topic_checklist_and_guide/) for an extremely extensive list).

## 2: Pick a Location

The next thing you should do is pick a location to set the game, even if you plan on letting your party travel. This is extremely important because _the Xeon Rapture Universe is designed to give you a very large array of elements to build your environment and story out of_.

There are **too many** elements in the XRU for you to use all of them in your story, and the world is designed that way: you should only use what you need! Each location in the XRU is made with certain rules and genre specifications in mind, and come packed with only a certain amount of elements to choose from, so if you pick a starting location first many of those initial choices will become much easier.

For example, if you plan on running a cyberpunky game with gritty characters who are looking to escape their situation, pick Valda; if you want to run a high fantasy game where you battle wyverns, evil wizards, and fight against oppressive kingdoms, pick Ydilik; if you want a space exploration game where you battle alien monsters, explore strange environments, and fight off pirates, pick the Kotharak Field.

Even if you end up leaving this initial location quickly it's important to have a starting point, if only just to give your players a place for their characters to have grown up in and an environment that has shaped them into the people they are today.

> [!NOTE] Important!
> But the biggest thing to remember is this: from the moment you start your game the worldbuilding is yours! I give you a bunch of details that you can use and some rules and guidelines to help your game feel like Xeon Rapture, but what you do with all of this is up to you!
>
> A lot of the worldbuilding that I give you, especially location and organization details, are *a little vague on purpose*. The idea is that the Isveh Supercluster is so massive, with so many different types of people, that everyone can do strong worldbuilding and create compelling details that fill out the world without necessarily stepping on any other XR creator's toes. Fleshing out the experience is up to you!

## 3: Choose Other Elements

After you've chosen a place for your adventure to begin you can choose what other elements you want to include. Each place includes options for threats and organizations you can use, as well as some themes that you can explore.

Make sure that you limit what you decide to focus on! Each location is massive, ranging from the size of a moon to a nebula tens of light-years wide. The purpose of this scale is to make the universe feel expansive and epic, not to overwhelm you, your characters, or the narrative with too many elements.

Consider focusing on one one or two threats and one or two orgs, especially right at the beginning. Feel free to shift focus to something else for one or two sessions or if you travel somewhere else, but the narrative through-line should be limited to just a handful of elements so that it can keep momentum and cohesion.

Think of your game a little like a TV show: each season has an arc, and within each arc sometimes your characters go off and have a little off-topic adventure, or get distracted for a couple episodes helping some random people, but in the end they come back to the narrative of the arc.

If your game is more like an episodic adventure show (with a new location and new problems to solve every couple of episodes) then you can hop from concept to concept as much as you'd like, but try to structure each "episode" with some sort of beginning, middle, and end, where you wrap up the plot threads fairly well at the end of each episode (maybe with some lingering plot hooks that you can pick up later).

> [!NOTE] My Experience GMing in the XRU
> The reason that I highlight only selecting a couple of elements to focus on at most is because _I've messed this up before_.
>
> I made the XRU and the XRRPG partly because I'm so entranced with so many different genres, ideas, and mechanics, so I made a world where all of these things can coexist.
>
> But when I first started GMing with the XRRPG I used too many of these elements in one game, making it really hard to manage and probably making it feel pretty scattered for my players.
>
> Learn from my mistakes! Keep it focused and your narrative and experience will be more impactful.

### Choosing What Mechanics to Include

The mechanics of the XRRPG are just as modular as the world itself is: there are many different sub-RPGs and rule modules that you can decide to use to give your players a taste of something new without leaving the Xeon Rapture Universe.

If you're running a sci-fi heavy game you might want to include the [[XRMECHA (Kids in Mechs)|XRMECHA]] system, which includes rules for running mech battles, or you may send your players into the web with the [[Webrunning 2.0|Webrunning]] sub-RPG, a roguelike system where you clear node after node and grow more powerful as you level up and get better gear drops.

If you're playing a more fantasy game you may want to include the [[xr/Worldbuilding/Lore/The Xenic Plane|Xenic Plane]] sub-RPG, a game set in the dreamworld of the XRU that has you rolling a dice pool to generate belief in order to achieve things that aren't even conceivable in the physical realm.

For rule modules we have a mini-system for [[Spaceship Rules|running a spaceship]], [[Drug Rules|taking drugs]], [[Reputation and Relationship Rules|keeping track of your relationships]], and [[The Stress Meter|pushing your characters to their breaking point]] (for a horror or horror-adjacent game).

If you want your players to be able to play fast and loose with danger without outright dying you can give them [[Spirit Anchors|spirit anchors]] that allow them to be revived, but at a price...for every revival they take a level of [[Spirit Decay|spirit decay]].

# Session 0

Session 0 is where you and your players get together to prep before you actually start the campaign. It's an optional but useful technique used to get everyone familiar with the system, with one another's characters, and the type of game you'll all be playing.

Here are some things you should try to get done during a session 0:

- Make sure you and your players' expectations for the game match up fairly well, or at least that you all know what you're getting into (see [[#1 Talk to Your Players|above]])
- If there are people in the group that don't know the system you'll be playing, make sure to outline it so that people at least get the basics (what dice to use, what rolls will look like, etc.)
- Character Creation
	- Help all the players get their characters made; point them to the [[Quick Start Guide]] and the [[Character Creation Template]] or the [website](https://app.xeonrapture.com)
	- Are any of the characters siblings or close friends? What other relationships do your characters live with?
	- What are the vibes of all the characters? What are their backstories? How can you weave their backstories into your narrative?
	- Does the party have diverse party composition? Game balance is very flexible in the XRRPG but it'll probably be more fun and more interesting if each character has different strengths and weaknesses.
- If you have some extra time at the end of session 0 feel free to jump into the game!
	- Do an opening scene where everyone meets.
	- If your characters grew up together do a flashback that helps your players figure out who their characters will end up being.
	- If you're feeling adventurous drop them right into combat or some other dangerous situation!

# Playing a Campaign

So you're all set up! You've talked to your players, gotten all of your worldbuilding in order, and played a Session 0 to get everyone on the same page. What now? What do you need in order to play a fun, engaging game of XRRPG?

Check out the [[XRRPG Cheatsheet]] for a handy reference sheet of all the XRRPG mechanics you'll need to remember, and make sure to read [[Tips for GMs]] for a bunch of ways to make a campaign smooth and satisfying for you and your players.

Above all remember that this is a game, you're here to have fun! Be willing to take risks and experiment in collaboration with your players.
