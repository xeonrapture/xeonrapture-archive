# Intro

You may ask, "how am I supposed to make a massive, multi-genre world feel cohesive? How do I get these different genre elements to feel like they're part of the same world without it seeming scattered and disjointed?"

Good question: this is one of the hardest tasks that I set out to solve with the XRU, and the solution is multifaceted and evolving. I have some thoughts about how to best make this work, but ultimately it's a question I'm still trying to fully wrap my mind around (so if you find something that works for you, please [tell me](https://forms.gle/Ptyf1a82r9AZLhgR7)!).

# Solution 1: Modularity

Probably the biggest thing you can do to make the XRU feel expansive but still manageable is by heavily relying on **modularity**: only use the elements you enjoy, and ignore the rest! 

For example, if you want to run a horror-adventure game with sci-fi elements you could set it in a sprawling, deserted space station and be battling the colony of vodarak (a species of bug people). In this case you would basically forget that the corps exist, forget that the gods exist, ignore spirits and devils completely. They're not part of your story, and they don't exist in or aren't focused on the location that you're in, so you don't need to worry about them at all.

This makes it much easier to both hone in on a tone you want and to not overwhelm yourself will all the XRU has to offer.

# Solution 2: Describing the Xeon

As for cohesion, the xeon is the binding element in the XRU, the distinctive flavor that makes it different from any other world or system, and because the xeon is everywhere and part of basically everything there will be some sort of visual and thematic throughline that you can rely on to help everything you do in the XRU feel part of the same world.

And each type of xeon looks the same no matter where you are, each color has consistent characteristics. Even with classes that don't use the fundamental powers tied to the xeon (spirit- or pact- focused classes such as warlocks and elementalists or enchanters, a class that uses color-mixed and whimsical xenic effects) you can use the visual language of the xeon to tie them to the basic worldbuilding.

So what does the xeon look like? Usually it's neon-y, with a glowing, energy-like vibe, and each color will have distinctive shape and motion. Blending different colors of xeon will mix their colors and shapes together, and interacting with the elements, spirits, or a pact can combine the look of a more traditional elemental or spiritual manipulation with the bright, color-forward look of xeon.

For example, if you're a mystic working with your spirits to enact certain effects, you can signal that these spirits are using the xeon to do their work by introducing those colors. Like if your mortal spirit uses the Soothe spell you can have it glow with green or amber light as it it heals your ally's wounds, or your dark spirit can clasp an enemy with blue and black energy as you Hamper them.

And a reminder: because xeon is everywhere, generated xeon (through powers, magic, tech, whatever) will react with and mix with the ambient xeon! So if someone is generating red and they're in a place with lots of green such as a jungle their red may get little fringes of orange, or if someone is generating green in a place with a lot of blue they may see cyan seep into their blue.

# Solution 3: Embrace Tone Shifting

When making the XRU I've tried to embrace all different types of both genres and also tones: bright, fun and even whimsical; deliberate and detailed in world- and magic-building; and even dark and brutal in the grim corners of the world. There are two ways to deal with this: only emphasize the tonal elements that you want in your game (which is a totally acceptable approach), or embrace a bit of the chaos (while still only including a limited amount of elements in your game).

In service of this I would suggest you try a bit of "fundark": a world that can be brutal and hard at times (or even most of the time) filled with characters that can have fun, be irreverent, and make warm and generous connections with each other and the NPCs around them. This is partly why [[XRRPG 2.0 Core Rules#Action Risk|Bit Risk]] exists, to make it easier for you to mess around a bit without it changing the fundamental consequences of the world around you.

Basically, allow the tone to shift when it needs to! Don't feel like it needs to be locked down to high fantasy, hard sci-fi, or grimdark. Lighten up when you need to, lock in when the plot requires it, engage in both brutal battles and dumb bits.

Of course whether this approach will work will depend on you, your party, and the game you want to play. But in a universe as diverse as the XRU (a world that is built to mirror the absurdity of the real world) I think it's worth it to at least try out a more relaxed, flexible, openminded approach.
