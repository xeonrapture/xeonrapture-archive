# Intro

Most people don't have strong powers or abilities like your characters will! Most people are normal and have limited power over their environment.

# Genre Theory

Genre = story type, and includes aesthetic rules, storytelling guidelines, themes, and often even a psychological or philosophical standpoint to make sure the genre vibe is imparted effectively.

For example:
- Cyberpunk is neon-washed, technologically advanced, godless, capitalistic, hedonistic, cynical, and morally gray. Often includes themes of the depravity of commodification and technological alienation.
- High fantasy is mystical and often includes elves, dwarves, magic, knights, and myths and legends. It often includes some sort of good vs evil narrative that ends with good winning the day.
- Horror is dark, nasty, and violent, with protagonists that have significantly less power than the villains, so much so that their main goal is simply survival. Themes often revolve around what a protagonist is willing to do to survive, the nature of villainy, and the fragile nature of mortality.

These genres offer a whole "package", a vibe that people gravitate towards because they depict a specific fantasy, reflect reality in a relevant way, or have some specific role that they play (e.g. horror is catharsis through terror, adventure is empowerment and thrill through an active and powerful protagonist, etc.).

Mixing genres is very common, and this mixing of genres can be messy especially when the genres are incompatible in aesthetic, attitude, and perspective. The way to make this work is to either pick two genres that are compatible already (such as grimdark and horror, or high fantasy and romance) or to make one genre bend to meet the other so that the piece of art can present a persuasive, cohesive whole.

A main goal of the XRU was to create a massive genre sandbox! Someone can tell a story that includes incredibly disparate elements with little to no whiplash because they all exist in the same universe with throughlines that tie it all together.

A big way that the XRU functions with multiple genres is by sectioning them off into different physical locations: Ydilik is fantasy, Rtolka-dala is steampunk, Valda is cyberpunk, Norryx is horror. Some of these places include rules to keep them fairly genre-pure (such as Ydilik not allowing tech and Valda not allowing magic), and other places allow more genre-mixing than others.

Experiment! Take elements of one genre and transplant them into another to create something new. Take the worldbuilding details of one genre and combine them with the storytelling rules of a different genre.

# How to Write a Scene

Below is a list of three things that every scene should include!

Not every "scene" necessarily includes these three things in the same amounts; every genre and every story will have a balance unique to itself. But I would argue that every good scene includes these three things at least to a certain extent.

## Things to Include
As long as every scene includes at least a bit of these three things, it's a good scene and should move the story forward.

### 1) Inner World
Every good scene should reveal something about a character or multiple characters' inner worlds, whether that be what they think about themselves, how they treat themselves, or their perception of the world.

This can include the character learning something new and progressing in their journey or messing up and regressing, but must feature some insight about the character.

This may only be implied! But it has to be new information that either we didn't have before or builds on information that we had in the past.

It may not include the character changing at all: some of the best scenes simply show *the lengths to which the character will go to accomplish their goal*. 

### 2) Outer World
Every good scene should reveal something about the world that the characters are in.

This can include the way society works, the way a character's relationships go, a magic system detail, something interesting about the science of the world, etc.

This may even mean revealing the rules about the story that the character is in, or what the storyteller is willing to do in a story.

Some stories may be more or less heavy on this point depending on how much worldbuilding needs to be done or how early you are in the story, but I would argue *every good scene needs this*. 

Sometimes Outer World means something like "What habits does the PC have?" or "What's a close relationship that the PC has?", questions that would seem like Inner World questions but don't really reveal anything interesting or deep about the character's thoughts and feelings and more does work in *situating the PC in the world itself*.

Because that's what Outer World is really about, after all: situating the PC, and as such the reader or participator, in the world and deepening their relationship with it.

### 3) Plot Point
This one is a must. Every good scene must involve a plot point.

This includes anything from a main character doing something to an NPC doing something that will prove consequential. It can also include a PC realizing something that will affect their actions or shape their worldview, so a revelation about the Inner World may also double as a Plot Point if it moves the story forward.

Always ask, "Why am I showing this scene in particular? What does this accomplish? Does it maintain my momentum?"
