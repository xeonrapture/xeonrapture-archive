# The Isveh Supercluster

All of this, all these mortals, every Archon and every dark god, every planet and every nebula is held within the Isveh Supercluster, a massive, billion-light-year-wide star structure containing around 10 quadrillion stars (10 with 15 zeros following it).

This supercluster is organized around the Center Group, a cluster of stars and other celestial objects that is rumored to be where the gods live. This star cluster has a very high concentration of xeon, and it's from this area that giant streams of red xeon (called starcurrents or simply currents) flow out to the rest of the supercluster. Along these currents stars form, as well as planets and other large terrestrial objects as the stars explode into colder, more complicated elements that condense from their own gravity.

Each current has a great stream of red xeon at its center, and sometimes blue streams form along asteroid fields, nebulas, or other massive amounts of cold matter. These currents, both red and blue, often break down into tributaries, getting smaller and smaller until they're gone. Within the currents themselves are lay lines, concentrations of xeon that can create [[Supercluster Portal Network#Interstellar Lay Line Portals|natural portals]] where they cross.

The more powerful gods (middle god and above) and groups of dragons are able to create stars, planets, and other astral objects themselves, and so they involve themselves directly in the shape of the universe.

# The Currents

There are twelve different currents, each branching off from the Center Group in a different direction, each of a different size and shape and character.

Some of these currents are incredibly unexplored, and with how much space and how many stars exist in these spaces, who knows what we might find!

Every so often a group of people load up a spacecraft or a caravan of spaceships and leave to make a colony in some faraway place. Some of these people are heard from again, but most aren't. Are they flourishing? Have they destroyed themselves? What have they found? We may never know.
